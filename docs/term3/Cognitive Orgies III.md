# OuterBody

OuterBody is an interactive wearable system that explores the relationship between body movement, sound, and collective energy production. 

The project investigates how kinetic movement generated while dancing can be transformed into reusable electrical energy through wearable inertial generators. At the same time, it reflects on nightlife culture as an energy-intensive environment where physical, emotional, and technological energies constantly interact.

The prototype uses body movement and step tracking to trigger audiovisual feedback in real time. As participants move, different layers of music and light are activated, transforming individual motion into a collective immersive experience.

The project combines wearable technology, club culture, speculative design, and sustainability to question how energy is produced, consumed, and perceived inside contemporary social spaces.

# Cognitive traces

During the development process, we shifted from thinking about energy only as electricity to understanding it as a broader phenomenon that includes emotional, bodily, social, and spatial dimensions. 

The project evolved from a simple technical experiment into a critical reflection on nightlife infrastructures and collective participation. We became interested in the paradox that clubs consume enormous amounts of energy while simultaneously generating intense bodily movement and social activation.

This led us to explore how wearable systems could make invisible energetic processes more tangible and visible through interaction.

# Moral traces

The project raised questions regarding sustainability and technological optimism. We reflected on the risk of presenting speculative technologies as fully sustainable solutions when the actual energy produced remains relatively small.

Instead of proposing the device as a realistic replacement for traditional energy infrastructures, we framed it as a critical and experiential tool capable of making energy consumption more visible and discussable.

The collaborative process was balanced among team members, and AI tools were mainly used as support for coding, troubleshooting, and brainstorming.

# Technical / process traces

The prototype combines motion sensing, real-time interaction, and wearable electronics. 

An ESP32 microcontroller and motion sensors were used to track body movement and send live data to TouchDesigner through OSC communication. Different sound layers and visual outputs were activated depending on the amount of movement generated by participants.

The wearable structure was designed to host inertial electromagnetic generators capable of converting kinetic movement into small amounts of electrical energy. The system was tested through iterative prototyping and multiple interaction experiments focused on dance and collective participation.
