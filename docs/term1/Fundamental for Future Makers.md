        <p><strong>Description</strong></p>
        <div class="col-scroll">
        <p>Coming from architecture and editorial design, I already knew how to 3D print and laser cut. But CNC 
  milling? Completely new. Touch Designer? Also new, and I absolutely loved it. Electronics are still very 
  new to me. Biomaterials? That's something I really want to explore more. Here's a quick rundown based on my 
  own projects—a butterfly and a frog model.</p>

<p><strong>Laser Cutting</strong><br>
Moved from random shapes to intentional design—margins, layers, spacing. Key lesson: always turn on 
ventilation first. I used this to cut parts for my butterfly project.</p>

<p><strong>3D Printing</strong><br>
I've used 3D printing before, but forgot a lot. This was a good refresher, especially learning Bambu Lab. 
For my project, I printed a frog model—came out better than expected. Next time I want to test organic 
shapes and supports.</p>

<p><strong>CNC Milling</strong><br>
First time, and honestly the scariest. I made a base for a 3d model. Failed a lot, learned even more: think 
about the tool's limits, bit size, material behavior, and always respect the 50% depth rule. This is where I 
failed most and learned most.</p>

<p><strong>Casting & Molding</strong><br>
We used a laser‑cut box as a mold, worried it wouldn't release.</p>

<p><strong>Touch Designer</strong><br>
I'd been curious for a while. The intro was quick but showed me what's possible. We made a basic animation. 
I really loved it and want to dive deeper.</p>

<p><strong>Biomaterials</strong><br>
We tried different recipes, used soft molds from the casting course, and got a messy but usable result. 
After this, I did a small workshop with Agnes at Valldaura—cooked with berries and acorns, tried bioprinting 
on a shirt. No sun, so the shirt failed, but now I'm hooked. I want to explore biomaterials much 
further—maybe even combine them with my frog and butterfly models.</p>

<p><strong>Electronics overall</strong><br>
Still very new to me. But the course made it less intimidating. I'll need more practice, but at least now 
I'm not afraid to try.</p></p>
